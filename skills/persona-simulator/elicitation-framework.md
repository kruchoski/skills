# Elicitation Framework

Structured questions to build persona fidelity across seven layers. Questions are organized by layer, with mode-specific additions where relevant.

## How to Use This Framework

1. **Automated data gathering first** - Run background-research to populate what's available
2. **Gap analysis** - Identify which layers have insufficient data
3. **Targeted elicitation** - Ask only questions for gaps that matter for the requested mode
4. **Mode-specific deep dive** - After core layers, ask mode-specific questions

**Principle:** Don't ask questions you can answer from data. Elicit only what's missing.

---

## Core Questions (All Modes)

### Layer 1: Professional/Public Identity

*Usually populated from background-research. Ask only if gaps exist.*

- What is their current role and organization?
- What's their career trajectory been?
- What are they known for professionally?
- What's their educational/credential background?
- What do they publish, speak about, or advocate publicly?

**Evidence quality:** HIGH confidence if from official sources (LinkedIn, org bio)

### Layer 2: Behavioral Patterns

*Requires direct observation. Ask to fill gaps.*

**Communication style:**
- How would you describe their communication style? (direct, diplomatic, verbose, concise)
- Do they prefer verbal or written communication?
- How do they structure arguments? (bottom-line up front? build to conclusion?)
- What phrases or language patterns do you notice them using?

**Decision-making:**
- How do they make decisions? (consensus-builder, decisive, analytical, intuitive)
- Do they decide quickly or deliberate?
- Do they seek input widely or trust their own judgment?
- How do they handle ambiguity?

**Meeting behavior:**
- Do they talk first or listen first in meetings?
- Do they ask questions or make statements?
- How do they handle being challenged or pushed back on?
- What topics make them visibly engaged vs. disengaged?

**Conflict handling:**
- How do they respond to disagreement?
- Do they address conflict directly or avoid it?
- How do they express frustration or displeasure?

**Evidence quality:** MODERATE confidence requires 2-3 observations

### Layer 3: Revealed Priorities

*Inferred from actions, not stated preferences. Observation-based.*

**Resource allocation:**
- What have you seen them actually fight for?
- Where do they spend discretionary time and attention?
- What do they let slide despite saying it matters?
- What topics do they return to unprompted?

**Decision history:**
- What decisions have you seen them make under pressure?
- What tradeoffs do they actually make (vs. claim to make)?
- What have they sacrificed for? What won't they sacrifice?

**Engagement patterns:**
- What makes them visibly engaged and energized?
- What makes them check out or disengage?
- What do they volunteer for vs. delegate away?

**Evidence quality:** MODERATE confidence requires pattern across decisions

### Layer 4: Situational Context

*Time-sensitive. Must be updated regularly.*

**Current pressures:**
- What pressures are they under right now?
- What deadlines or deliverables are they facing?
- What resource constraints are they operating under?

**Recent history:**
- Have they had any recent wins that affect their standing?
- Have they had any recent setbacks or failures?
- Has anything changed in their role or organization recently?

**Near-term focus:**
- What are they trying to accomplish in the next 30-90 days?
- What's on their radar that might not be public?
- What upcoming events might be on their mind?

**Political dynamics:**
- What organizational currents might be affecting them?
- Who are their allies and adversaries?
- What power dynamics are they navigating?

**Evidence quality:** LOW to MODERATE - highly perishable

### Layer 5: Relational Dynamics

*the user's direct knowledge of the relationship.*

**History:**
- How did you meet? What's the history?
- How often do you interact? In what contexts?
- What have your interactions been like? (positive, neutral, friction)

**Perception:**
- How do they seem to perceive your credibility?
- What role do they see you playing? (peer, expert, subordinate, ally)
- What do they seem to want from you? (support, information, challenge, validation)

**Power dynamics:**
- What's the power relationship? (peer, superior, subordinate, client, external)
- Who needs whom more in this relationship?
- Are there any asymmetries that affect the dynamic?

**Trust trajectory:**
- How has the relationship been trending? (building, stable, eroding)
- Have there been any trust-building or trust-damaging moments?
- What would strengthen or weaken the relationship?

**Evidence quality:** MODERATE - the user's interpretation

### Layer 6: Known Blindspots and Biases

*Requires accumulated observation. Often LOW confidence.*

**Cognitive patterns:**
- What do they consistently get wrong?
- What do they tend to underweight or dismiss?
- What are they overconfident about?
- What blindspots might come from their expertise area?

**Structural biases:**
- How might their role/incentives distort their view?
- What biases might come from their career path?
- What perspectives are they unlikely to have been exposed to?

**Historical patterns:**
- When have they been wrong in the past?
- What types of mistakes do they tend to make?
- What feedback have they been resistant to?

**Evidence quality:** LOW - requires many observations

### Knowledge Boundaries

*Prevents the model from filling gaps with generic "expert" behavior. Ask to fill gaps.*

- What topics would they defer on? Where would they say "that's not my area"?
- What are the limits of their professional experience? What developments or domains are outside their direct knowledge?
- How do they characteristically signal uncertainty? Do they hedge, redirect, or openly acknowledge what they don't know?
- Is there a temporal boundary to their expertise? (e.g., deep experience through 2020 but hasn't kept up with recent developments in a specific area)

**Evidence quality:** MODERATE - requires observing how they handle edge-of-expertise situations

### Example Outputs

*Capture 2-4 examples of how this person actually communicates. These anchor voice and reasoning style more effectively than trait descriptions.*

- Can you share an example of how they'd respond to a new proposal or idea? What would their first reaction look like?
- Can you give me an example of how they deliver constructive feedback? What does their critique sound like?
- When they disagree with something, what does that look like? Can you describe or quote a specific instance?
- What does their advice typically sound like — can you give me an example of guidance they've given you?

**Evidence quality:** HIGH when based on direct quotes or recalled exchanges; MODERATE when paraphrased

## Mode-Specific Questions

### Advisory Mode (Layer 7: Advisory Profile)

*Required for Advisory mode. Captures how they coach/advise.*

**Coaching style:**
- How does [Name] typically give you feedback?
  - Direct and blunt?
  - Socratic (questions that lead you to answers)?
  - Supportive framing first, then challenge?
  - Structured frameworks?
- Do they tell you what to do or help you figure it out yourself?
- How do they balance support with challenge?

**Mental models:**
- What frameworks or mental models do they frequently reference?
- What principles seem to guide their advice?
- What analogies or stories do they use?
- What questions do they consistently ask?

**Experience base:**
- What experiences shape their perspective on this topic?
- What have they done that informs their advice?
- What mistakes have they made that they warn against?
- What successes do they draw on?

**Push and pull patterns:**
- What do they usually push you on or challenge?
- What instincts of yours do they typically affirm?
- What topics do they have strong opinions on?
- What do they leave for you to decide?

**Advice track record:**
- When you've taken their advice, how has it worked out?
- What's the best advice they've ever given you?
- Have they ever steered you wrong? How?
- What do they NOT give good advice on?

**Availability and rhythm:**
- How do they prefer to give advice? (formal meetings, informal, async)
- How responsive are they when you need guidance?
- Do they check in proactively or wait to be asked?

### Anticipatory Mode (Evaluator Profile)

*Required for Anticipatory mode. Captures how they evaluate.*

**Evaluation criteria:**
- What do they evaluate proposals against?
- What are their must-haves vs. nice-to-haves?
- What's their risk tolerance?
- What outcomes do they care about most?

**Question patterns:**
- What questions do they typically ask when evaluating?
- What do they probe on most?
- What gaps do they always notice?
- What do they rarely ask about (blind spots)?

**Confidence signals:**
- What makes them skeptical?
- What builds their confidence?
- What would make them say yes? Say no?
- What evidence do they find compelling?

**Concern expression:**
- How do they signal approval? (words, body language, actions)
- How do they signal concern? (direct, indirect, questions)
- How do they express disagreement?
- What does silence from them mean?

**Decision behavior:**
- Do they decide in the room or need time?
- Do they consult others before deciding?
- How do they communicate decisions?
- What would change their mind after a decision?

### Collaborative Mode (Partner Profile)

*Required for Collaborative mode. Captures how they coordinate.*

**Working style:**
- How do they like to work together? (divide and conquer, constant sync, async)
- What's their communication rhythm preference?
- How often do they want to sync?
- How do they prefer to handle handoffs?

**Responsibility division:**
- What decisions do they want input on?
- What do they prefer to handle independently?
- How do they like to divide responsibilities?
- What do they want ownership of vs. delegate?

**Coordination mechanics:**
- How do they track shared work?
- How do they handle dependencies?
- How do they manage timelines?
- What tools/processes do they prefer?

**Disagreement handling:**
- How do they handle disagreement with teammates?
- Do they prefer to resolve privately or openly?
- What does healthy conflict look like with them?
- What triggers defensiveness?

**Collaboration history:**
- Where have you collaborated well in the past?
- Where has there been friction?
- What's worked in similar partnerships?
- What hasn't worked?

**Strengths and gaps:**
- What do they bring that complements your strengths?
- Where might your approaches conflict?
- What should you defer to them on?
- What should they defer to you on?

## Elicitation Best Practices

### Question Sequencing

1. **Start broad:** "How would you describe their communication style?"
2. **Get specific:** "Can you give me an example of that?"
3. **Probe for patterns:** "Is that consistent or situational?"
4. **Check confidence:** "How confident are you in that assessment?"

### Evidence Prompting

When user provides assessment, probe for evidence:
- "What have you observed that leads you to that conclusion?"
- "How many times have you seen that behavior?"
- "Is that based on direct observation or what others have said?"
- "How recently was that?"

### Confidence Calibration

Ask user to rate their confidence:
- "On a scale of high/medium/low, how confident are you in that?"
- "Is that based on multiple observations or a single instance?"
- "How might you be wrong about this?"

### Gap Acknowledgment

When data is insufficient:
- "I don't have enough to model this layer confidently. Should we proceed with uncertainty or gather more data?"
- "This is speculative based on limited information. The simulation will flag this as low confidence."

## Mode Selection Questions

If mode is ambiguous, ask:

**Understanding the need:**
- "What are you hoping to get from this simulation?"
- "What decision or action will this inform?"

**Relationship context:**
- "Is this someone who advises you, evaluates your work, or works alongside you?"
- "Are you preparing to receive something from them (advice, judgment) or coordinate with them?"

**Stance clarification:**
- "Do you want them to coach you, assess something, or align with you?"

**Direct mode question:**
- "Should I simulate them as:
  - An **advisor** giving you guidance? (Advisory)
  - A **stakeholder** reacting to what you present? (Anticipatory)
  - A **partner** coordinating with you? (Collaborative)"
